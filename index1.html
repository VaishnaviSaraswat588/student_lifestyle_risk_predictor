Confusion matrix on a held-out 20,000-row test set (20% split).

Model: logistic regression (scikit-learn), features standardized, class_weight="balanced" to compensate for depression being only ~10% of rows.

Features: sleep duration, study hours, social media hours, physical activity, stress level, an engineered "high stress" flag (stress ≥ 8, added because the raw data shows depression risk roughly quadruples at that threshold rather than rising smoothly), CGPA, age, gender, and department (one-hot).

Strongest signal: CGPA and the high-stress flag dominate the standardized coefficients — lower CGPA and crossing the stress-8 threshold are the two biggest movers. Sleep duration has a smaller but consistent negative effect. Department and gender contribute almost nothing.

Why recall over accuracy: for a health-adjacent risk flag, missing true cases (false negatives) is costlier than over-flagging, so the model is tuned to catch ~68% of real depression cases at the cost of a higher false-positive rate — a deliberate precision/recall trade-off, not an accident.

Caveat: ROC-AUC of 0.69 means the model is meaningfully better than chance but far from a reliable individual-level predictor. Real mental health risk depends on far more than eight lifestyle numbers.

Built as a portfolio case study on a 100,000-row student lifestyle dataset. Model trained in Python (scikit-learn), exported to run entirely client-side — no backend, no data leaves your browser.
